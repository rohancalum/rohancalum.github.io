  • Making sustainability data science easier

    In the summer of 2018, I was hired to kick-start data science efforts for the University of British Columbia's Sustainability Office. However, I found it difficult to find and access the datasets I needed. To solve this, I designed and led my team to launch an open data portal (built using DKAN). The platform now serves various sustainability-related datasets managed by the university to the public.

    Key Learnings:

    • Don't hesitate to speak up and seek clarification early when things don't make sense.
    • Ensure everyone is using the same vocabulary (and dictionary) in meetings.
    • More demos, less words. A prototype or wireframe is the most efficient way of explaining the value of a new tool or service.
    • Sayre's law and the incentive/power structures of public institutions can stifle operational efficiency and make innovation harder. The solution is probably more demos, less words.

  • Who are the best people to ask how to improve education?

    People who have experienced its consequences. I've long been an advocate for strong education systems (I mean, who isn't?). Aside from chance encounters, they are probably the single most important determinant of life paths. Yet, when was the last time you were asked for your perspective on how Grade 10 could be improved? When I heard that Let's Talk Science was consulting K-12 students across the country for their Canada 2067 initiative, I felt the need to ensure post-secondary students and young professionals could provide their input. After all, hindsight is 20/20. As a member of the World Economic Forum Global Shapers Community, I coordinated roundtable discussions with over 200 young people from around the country. The outcomes were presented to around 500 educational leaders and stakeholders.

    Key Learnings:

    • The WEF Global Shapers Community is a fantastic venue for getting projects like this done efficiently.
    • If you feel like you have something useful to add, and the capacity to add it, people are usually happy to hear from you. It's better to co-create than to critique.

  • A film to make you think differently about youth homelessness

    In high school, I'd sometimes hear classmates speak poorly of homeless youth. This bothered me, so I worked with the City of Edmonton and Akash Sherman to try and change perspectives. We collaborated with local community service organizations for months, and partnered with five incredible youth to share their experiences through film. The plan was for this to seed profound change, but we fell short. Many kids are still stuck in systemic homelessness. This work left much to be desired, but I hope by sharing it new conversations and ideas can be sparked. You can watch the full film here.

    Key Learnings:

    • The best person to deliver on your vision, is you.
    • Push authorities and power to be better, but articulate how they can be better, and don't easily take no for an answer.
    • There are many lost feedback signals in society (unaccounted externalities and antagonisms) which result in cost inefficiencies. Homelessness is one of them.
    • Lack of coordination among service providers and excessive bureaucracy has created an entire service "industry" around treating the symptoms homelessness.
